This release is a fictional repeatability validation.

It is not:

- Actual client work.
- Actual operating use.
- A production pilot.
- A workload or time study.
- A human-adoption assessment.
- A software or workflow test.
- A legal or regulatory review.

## 10. Repeatability Design Principles

The validation shall follow these principles:

1. Independence.
   - New facts shall not merely rename the Release 2.0 facts.
   - Each scenario shall introduce materially different operating conditions.

2. Comparability.
   - Each scenario shall preserve the primary control question of its comparator family.

3. Proportionality.
   - Low-complexity scenarios may combine roles or gates when all requirements remain satisfied.
   - Higher-risk scenarios shall show the additional review, hold, escalation, evidence, or closure requirements caused by the facts.

4. No inferred authority.
   - Authority shall be identified or the matter shall remain unresolved or on hold.

5. No outcome forcing.
   - The scenario outcome shall follow the facts and controlling requirements.
   - The validation shall not force a pass merely to preserve the Release 2.0 result.

6. Evidence sufficiency.
   - Every material conclusion shall identify supporting evidence.

7. Traceability.
   - Status, state, version, authority, issuance, notification, acceptance, and closure history shall remain reconstructable.

8. Boundary preservation.
   - The procedure shall coordinate other governance domains without replacing their controlling decisions.

9. Defect discipline.
   - Execution effort, length, inconvenience, or preference shall not be classified as a defect without control failure.

10. Aid discipline.
    - No reusable aid shall be recommended without the approved repeated-control-problem threshold.

11. Confidentiality.
    - Scenarios shall use invented entities and facts.
    - No actual confidential, regulated, client, partner, or personal information shall be used.

12. Publication deferral.
    - No production Word, PDF, or PowerPoint governance artifact shall be created.

## 12. Required Scenario Set

### 12.1 R-01 - Routine Final Deliverable Release

Use a low-complexity final deliverable with:

- Clear engagement scope.
- Clear evidence source.
- A named authorized recipient.
- A defined advisory purpose.
- No partner contribution.
- No material commercial commitment.
- A controlled client portal or other delivery method different from S-01.
- A receipt requirement but no formal acceptance requirement.

Required test:

- Proportionate role and gate combination.
- Final-status determination.
- Release authority.
- Publication handoff.
- Controlled issuance.
- Delivery and receipt evidence.
- Record closure.

### 12.2 R-02 - Client Review Copy

Use a professionally formatted review copy with:

- Unresolved factual items.
- More than one proposed client reviewer.
- A defined comment period.
- A risk that the material could be mistaken for an approved executive recommendation.
- Review-copy marking and communication.
- No authorization for final reliance, quotation, redistribution, or decision use.

Required test:

- Review-copy status.
- Recipient authorization.
- Reliance and redistribution limits.
- Review feedback handling.
- No inferred final status.
- Deferred closure.
- Transition to revision, final review, withdrawal, or no further release.

### 12.3 R-03 - Release Hold

Use a completed deliverable with a different combination of unresolved conditions, such as:

- Unclear signature or release authority.
- Proposed delivery to an external contractor.
- A subcontractor or partner-supplied section.
- An unsupported outcome, savings, certification, or assurance statement.
- Confidentiality or need-to-know uncertainty.
- Schedule pressure.

Required test:

- Immediate release hold.
- Commercial, confidentiality, partner, quality, engagement, publication, and governance handoffs as applicable.
- Resolution, limitation, removal, escalation, rejection, or continued hold.
- No release while a material condition remains unresolved.
- Complete hold and resolution evidence.

### 12.4 R-04 - Material Change After Approval

Use an approved but unissued deliverable where new evidence changes:

- A finding.
- A recommendation.
- A priority.
- A timeline.
- A risk statement.
- A recipient reliance condition.

Required test:

- Stop scheduled issuance.
- Preserve the prior approved version.
- Assess materiality.
- Invalidate or limit stale approval.
- Assign a new version.
- Repeat affected review.
- Regenerate and revalidate the publication output.
- Obtain a new release decision.
- Issue only the newly approved version.

### 12.5 R-05 - Post-Issuance Failure

Use an issued deliverable with a materially different post-issuance failure, such as:

- A client portal link shared with an unauthorized external collaborator.
- A permissions error that allowed access beyond the approved recipient group.
- A material maturity score, date, scope statement, or recommendation error.
- A need to revoke access, limit reliance, notify affected recipients, correct the controlled source, and issue a replacement.

Required test:

- Immediate containment.
- Evidence preservation.
- Cross-policy routing.
- Withdrawal, correction, supersession, replacement, or limitation decision.
- Recipient notification.
- Corrected-source review.
- New publication and release decision.
- Residual-risk acceptance.
- Closure and reopening criteria.

### 12.6 R-06 - Open Acceptance or Reliance Issue

Use an issued deliverable where:

- Delivery and receipt are confirmed.
- Formal acceptance is required.
- The client questions whether a milestone, outcome, dependency, or estimate is a binding promise.
- The issue could affect reliance, payment, obligation, or engagement closeout.
- No factual defect is initially confirmed.

Required test:

- Receipt, acknowledgment, and acceptance distinction.
- Hold on deliverable and engagement closure.
- Commercial and engagement review.
- Clarification, correction, replacement, or limitation decision.
- Client communication.
- Acknowledgment and acceptance evidence.
- Closure only after all material conditions are resolved.

## 14. Consistency Assessment Method

Each scenario shall receive a consistency assessment across these dimensions:

| Dimension | Required assessment |
| --- | --- |
| Status | Is the deliverable status supported and consistent with the facts? |
| State | Is the operating-process state supported and distinct from status? |
| Authority | Is authority identified, limited, or held unresolved without inference? |
| Purpose and use | Are purpose, audience, intended use, and reliance controlled? |
| Recipient | Is recipient authorization and need-to-know addressed? |
| Reviews | Are required subject-matter reviews coordinated proportionately? |
| Gates | Are all applicable gates satisfied, held, rejected, limited, or documented as not applicable? |
| Version | Is source, version, change, approval, and issuance history reconstructable? |
| Release | Is the release decision supported by the appropriate authority? |
| Issuance | Is only the approved version issued through the approved method? |
| Post-issuance | Are containment, correction, withdrawal, replacement, limitation, and notification handled appropriately? |
| Acceptance | Are receipt, review, acknowledgment, and acceptance kept distinct? |
| Closure | Are open issues resolved or appropriately transferred before closure? |
| Reopening | Are later facts capable of reopening the record? |
| Evidence | Is the required record complete and traceable? |
| Boundaries | Are other governance domains coordinated without duplication? |

The scenario shall then be classified:

- Materially consistent.
- Materially consistent with proportionate variation.
- Inconsistent but supportable after clarification.
- Materially inconsistent.
- Unable to determine.

Any result other than materially consistent or materially consistent with proportionate variation requires a documented finding and disposition.

## 17. Procedure-Amendment Threshold

A procedure amendment may be recommended only when evidence demonstrates at least one of the following:

- Contradictory instructions.
- A missing required authority or decision.
- A missing required status or state transition.
- An unworkable control gate.
- A material boundary conflict.
- A required action with no executable path.
- Repeated inconsistent outcomes caused by the procedure.
- Repeated premature closure caused by the procedure.
- Repeated recipient error caused by the procedure.
- Repeated evidence or traceability loss caused by the procedure.
- A continuing policy requirement that is not operationalized.

Procedure length, manual coordination, administrative effort, preference, or one difficult scenario is not sufficient.

Any amendment requires separately approved implementation scope unless the approved Release 2.1 plan is formally amended before implementation.

## 18. Execution-Aid Threshold

A reusable execution aid may be recommended only when the same control problem appears in at least two independent scenarios and causes one or more of the following:

- Repeated field omission.
- Inconsistent status selection.
- Inconsistent state selection.
- Duplicate authoritative records.
- Improper approval.
- Improper release.
- Recipient error.
- Missing or late closure criteria.
- Lost authority traceability.
- Lost evidence traceability.
- Unassigned post-issuance action.
- Repeated inability to identify authority.
- Repeated inability to reconstruct release evidence.
- Repeated manual failure that a proportionate aid would control.

An aid shall not be recommended merely because:

- The procedure is long.
- The record contains many fields.
- Coordination is manual.
- A checklist would be convenient.
- Software could automate the work.
- One scenario was difficult.
- A reviewer prefers a template.

Any future aid requires separately approved scope, creation, validation, and operating-use testing.

## 19. Training-Need Threshold

A separate training need may be recommended only when repeated evidence demonstrates:

- Misunderstanding of status versus state.
- Misunderstanding of creation, publication, and release authority.
- Misunderstanding of receipt versus acceptance.
- Repeated bypass of required gates.
- Repeated failure to preserve evidence.
- Repeated premature closure.
- Repeated cross-policy routing failure.
- Repeated misapplication that is not caused by a procedure defect.

Normal procedure familiarization is not a separate training requirement.

Any training artifact requires separately approved scope.
