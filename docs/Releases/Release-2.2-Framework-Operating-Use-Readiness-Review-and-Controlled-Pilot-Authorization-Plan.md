## 1. Purpose

This plan defines a controlled Release 2.2 review to determine whether the Send Manna Too LLC Business Governance Framework is sufficiently complete, coherent, and operationally bounded to authorize one narrowly scoped actual operating-use pilot.

The release will document the framework-level readiness decision and, only if supported by evidence, authorize a future pilot under explicit eligibility criteria, exclusions, stop conditions, evidence requirements, and governance boundaries.

Release 2.2 will not execute client work, conduct the pilot, create reusable operating aids, or begin production publication.

## 4. Release Objective

Determine and document whether the framework:

1. Contains sufficient approved authority, policy, procedure, evidence, publication, and closeout controls to support one narrowly scoped actual operating-use pilot.
2. Contains any unresolved lifecycle gap that blocks a pilot.
3. Contains any carryforward that blocks a pilot.
4. Can collect useful actual operating evidence without creating premature forms, trackers, registers, workflows, ticketing requirements, training artifacts, or production publication outputs.
5. Can preserve confidentiality, authority, scope, records, release, and acceptance boundaries during a pilot.
6. Is ready for controlled rollout planning after pilot evidence is obtained.
7. Should continue deferring end-stage production publication.
8. Requires any additional fictional validation before actual use.

## 8. Readiness Decision Categories

The Release 2.2 record shall use one of the following final readiness determinations:

| Determination | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Not ready | A material control or authority gap blocks even a narrow pilot |
| Conditionally ready | One narrowly bounded pilot may be authorized subject to all stated conditions |
| Ready for controlled rollout planning only | Planning may begin, but actual pilot or rollout remains unauthorized |
| Ready for broader controlled rollout | Sufficient actual evidence exists for broader use; this outcome is not expected from Release 2.2 alone |

Release 2.2 shall not select a more favorable category than the evidence supports.

## 9. Minimum Pilot Eligibility Criteria

A future pilot may be authorized only if all applicable criteria are satisfied:

1. The engagement is low complexity and limited in duration.
2. The client or contracting party is clearly identified.
3. The requesting and approving parties are sufficiently understood.
4. Written authorization and scope evidence exist.
5. Expected deliverables are identifiable.
6. Client and firm responsibilities are identifiable.
7. Material assumptions, dependencies, constraints, and exclusions are documented.
8. The commercial arrangement is standard or has completed appropriate review.
9. No unusual liability, indemnity, warranty, guarantee, insurance, audit, security, regulatory, tax, or professional obligation is accepted without qualified review.
10. No third party, subcontractor, referral partner, external collaborator, or material vendor dependency participates in delivery.
11. No regulated personal information, privileged information, highly sensitive client evidence, production credentials, secrets, or unnecessary personal information is required.
12. Information use, access, sharing, and disclosure can be limited to an authorized need-to-know purpose.
13. One clearly defined external deliverable can be governed through the approved lifecycle.
14. Release authority, recipients, intended use, reliance, and redistribution conditions can be identified.
15. Client review, acknowledgment, acceptance, or completion expectations can be identified.
16. Required records can be preserved in an identifiable and recoverable authoritative source or traceable set of records.
17. No record disposition is required during the pilot.
18. The owner can pause, escalate, or terminate affected activity when a stop condition occurs.
19. The pilot can be completed without creating a reusable controlled execution aid.
20. The pilot can be conducted without production publication of the entire framework.

## 10. Prohibited Pilot Characteristics

A future pilot shall not proceed when it requires or materially involves:

1. Unclear engagement, commercial, signature, disclosure, release, or acceptance authority.
2. Highly negotiated or materially unusual contract terms without qualified review.
3. Regulated, privileged, highly sensitive, or security-sensitive information beyond the approved operating boundary.
4. Subcontractor, partner, referral, vendor, or external-collaborator delivery involvement.
5. Legal, tax, insurance, regulatory, certification, attestation, audit-opinion, or other professional conclusions outside authorized competence.
6. Guaranteed outcomes, unsupported promises, warranties, or unlimited obligations.
7. Material client reliance that cannot be bounded or explained.
8. Record destruction, deletion, disposition, or retention-schedule implementation.
9. Technical control operation not governed by approved technical standards.
10. Activity that cannot be paused safely when authority, scope, evidence, or confidentiality is unclear.
11. A requirement for reusable forms, trackers, software, automation, or training before the pilot can be controlled.
12. Any condition that would create unmanaged legal, contractual, confidentiality, commercial, evidence, client-trust, or reputational exposure.

## 11. Pilot Stop and Escalation Conditions

A future pilot shall pause affected activity when:

1. Authorization is absent, withdrawn, inconsistent, or unclear.
2. Scope, deliverables, timing, responsibilities, pricing, or obligations materially change without approval.
3. A client requests work or commitments outside the approved scope.
4. A requested deliverable cannot be supported by sufficient evidence.
5. Confidentiality status, disclosure authority, recipient need-to-know, or information sensitivity is unclear.
6. Third-party involvement is proposed or discovered.
7. Material contract, legal, insurance, tax, regulatory, or professional-review needs arise.
8. Release authority, final status, recipient, reliance, redistribution, or acceptance conditions are unresolved.
9. A material error, misdirection, unauthorized release, obsolete version, or reliance concern is identified.
10. Required records are missing, altered, unrecoverable, duplicated without control, or maintained only in an unstable location.
11. Continued activity could materially increase exposure.
12. The matter exceeds the owner's approved authority or competence.

The applicable approved procedure shall govern escalation, containment, corrective action, closure, and reopening.

## 12. Required Actual Operating-Use Evidence

A future pilot record shall collect, at minimum:

| Evidence Area | Minimum Evidence |
| --- | --- |
| Engagement context | Client or contracting party, purpose, scope, owners, authorization, assumptions, dependencies, exclusions, and expected deliverables |
| Lifecycle execution | Actual states, gates, decisions, holds, escalations, changes, acceptance, and closeout |
| Time and workload | Elapsed time and material effort by major lifecycle stage or control gate |
| Completeness | Information initially missing, added later, or repeatedly requested |
| Authority | Decisions requiring clarification, escalation, or qualified review |
| Commercial control | Scope, pricing, obligation, signature, and change-control evidence |
| Information handling | Information categories used, need-to-know decisions, minimization, redaction, disclosure, and access limits |
| Third-party control | Confirmation that no unapproved third party participated |
| Deliverable control | Source, version, review, approval, publication validation, release authority, recipients, issuance, delivery, correction, or replacement |
| Client response | Receipt, review, acknowledgment, acceptance, dispute, or no-response treatment |
| Records and evidence | Authoritative source, traceability, preservation, confidentiality, recoverability, and duplication control |
| Friction and rework | Manual coordination, repeated review, navigation difficulty, duplicated effort, or unclear language |
| Deviations and corrective action | Exceptions, noncompliance, containment, corrective action, validation, residual risk, and closure |
| Outcome | Completion, acceptance, closure, unresolved matters, and residual risk |
| Improvement evidence | Repeated omission, inconsistency, delay, control failure, or justified need for a narrow execution aid or document clarification |

1. Operator learning issue.
2. One-time execution friction.
3. Repeated execution friction.
4. Evidence-system limitation.
5. Procedure ambiguity.
6. Policy ambiguity.
7. Authority ambiguity.
8. Boundary ambiguity.
9. Missing operating method.
10. Training need.
11. Execution-aid candidate.
12. Tooling candidate.
13. Client-behavior dependency.
14. Commercial or contractual dependency.
15. Information-handling dependency.
16. Records or recoverability dependency.
17. No defect.

A proposed change or execution aid shall require evidence of a recurring or material control problem. Convenience alone is insufficient.

## 14. Pilot Authorization Decision Gate

Release 2.2 may authorize one future pilot in principle only when the release record confirms:

1. No unresolved authority gap blocks the pilot.
2. No missing policy or procedure blocks the pilot.
3. Remaining domain gaps can be eliminated or bounded through pilot eligibility criteria.
4. Existing procedures can govern the lifecycle without amendment.
5. Existing records can preserve sufficient evidence without a new controlled form or system.
6. No production framework publication is required before the pilot.
7. Stop and escalation conditions are defined.
8. Actual evidence requirements are defined.
9. Pilot scope remains narrower than broader rollout.
10. The final decision is approved and recorded in the Decision Register.

Authorization in principle shall not select an actual client, start an engagement, or bypass ordinary engagement and commercial authorization.

## 15. Future Pilot Start Gate

## 16. Broader Rollout Decision Boundary

Release 2.2 shall not authorize broad rollout.

Broader rollout should require actual pilot evidence demonstrating:

1. Procedure executability in real operating conditions.
2. Acceptable workload and completion time.
3. Reliable authority and decision paths.
4. Adequate evidence-system performance.
5. Acceptable confidentiality and records handling.
6. Reliable deliverable release and acceptance control.
7. Manageable execution friction.
8. No unresolved material policy or procedure defect.
9. Any justified training or execution aid has been separately approved and validated.
10. Production publication and controlled distribution are ready or appropriately scheduled.

## 17. Production Publication Decision Boundary

Production publication remains deferred under DEC-0011 unless Release 2.2 identifies compelling evidence to change the roadmap.

Release 2.2 shall presume continued deferral because:

1. Actual operating evidence has not yet been collected.
2. Pilot evidence may identify narrow clarifications or justified operating aids.
3. Incremental production publication would increase regeneration, revalidation, checksum, manifest, and supersession effort.
4. Markdown in Git remains canonical.
5. End-stage batch publication remains the approved method before final framework closeout or controlled rollout.
