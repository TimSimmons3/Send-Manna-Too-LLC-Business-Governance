## 1. Purpose

This policy defines governance requirements for records, evidence, retention, preservation, recoverability, review, and authorized disposition within the Send Manna Too LLC Business Governance Framework.

The purpose of this policy is to preserve reliable evidence, support auditability and continuity, protect confidential information, prevent uncontrolled retention or premature disposition, and ensure that material business and governance activity remains appropriately documented.

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to governance records, client engagement records, commercial and contracting records, confidentiality-related records, third-party and partner records, publication records, release records, operational records, and supporting evidence maintained by or for Send Manna Too LLC.

This policy applies throughout the record lifecycle, including identification, creation, receipt, categorization, status determination, use, preservation, retention review, recoverability, and authorized disposition.

This policy does not create a detailed retention schedule, fixed retention periods, legal advice, statutory retention conclusions, litigation or discovery procedures, technical storage architecture, backup schedules, disaster recovery procedures, folder structures, records-management software requirements, or detailed deletion and destruction procedures.

## 3. Policy Statement

Send Manna Too LLC shall manage records and evidence through controlled governance practices that support integrity, traceability, confidentiality, accountability, recoverability, business continuity, and appropriate disposition.

Records shall be retained for an approved purpose and for no longer than justified by applicable obligations, governance needs, client needs, commercial needs, evidence requirements, risk considerations, or business continuity needs.

No record shall be altered, concealed, destroyed, disposed of, or allowed to become unrecoverable in a manner that compromises legal or contractual obligations, client trust, confidentiality, governance accountability, evidence quality, or firm integrity.

## 4. Records and Evidence Governance Principles

Records and evidence governance shall be guided by the following principles:

- Material decisions, approvals, commitments, exceptions, reviews, changes, and completed activities must be supported by appropriate evidence.
- Records must be sufficient to explain what occurred, when it occurred, who was responsible, what authority applied, and what evidence supported the outcome.
- Record status and authoritative source must be clear where ambiguity could affect accountability or reliance.
- Evidence must remain accurate, understandable, traceable, and protected from unauthorized alteration or loss.
- Retention must be based on documented and supportable criteria.
- Confidentiality and need-to-know requirements continue throughout the record lifecycle.
- Required records must remain reasonably recoverable for their approved retention period.
- Unnecessary duplication and unmanaged over-retention must be avoided.
- Disposition must be authorized, controlled, and suspended when preservation is required.
- Domain-specific policies remain authoritative for identifying the records and evidence required within their scope.

## 5. Record Categories and Status

Records and related materials may include:

- Authoritative records: Approved sources relied upon to establish a decision, obligation, approval, transaction, commitment, change, exception, or completed activity.
- Supporting evidence: Materials that substantiate or explain an authoritative record, decision, review, or conclusion.
- Working materials: Drafts, notes, analyses, intermediate outputs, or temporary materials used to prepare an approved record or deliverable.
- Publication outputs: Word, PDF, PowerPoint, or other formatted outputs produced from an approved source for distribution or presentation.
- Convenience copies: Duplicate copies maintained for temporary access or operational convenience.
- Reusable templates: Approved aids used to create future records but which do not prove that a specific activity occurred.

Record category and status shall be determined by purpose and approved use, not merely by filename, format, location, or possession.

A working draft, convenience copy, template, or publication output shall not be treated as the authoritative record unless an approved governance decision or applicable business requirement establishes that status.

## 6. Record Identification and Ownership

Material records shall have a responsible owner, business function, engagement, release, or governance process that is accountable for their appropriate management.

Record ownership includes responsibility for:

- Identifying the record purpose and status.
- Confirming the appropriate authoritative source.
- Protecting confidentiality and authorized access.
- Preserving required context and supporting evidence.
- Identifying applicable retention considerations.
- Supporting periodic review and authorized disposition.
- Escalating uncertainty, conflict, loss, or integrity concerns.

Record ownership does not permit an owner to disregard higher-level governance, contractual commitments, client requirements, confidentiality restrictions, or applicable legal obligations.

## 7. Authoritative Records and Supporting Materials

The authoritative record shall be identifiable when multiple drafts, versions, copies, systems, or publication outputs exist.

Markdown in Git remains the canonical source for framework documents. Git remains the authoritative version history for those documents.

Client, commercial, contractual, partner, financial, operational, or other business records may have a different approved authoritative source appropriate to their purpose and governing obligation.

Word, PDF, PowerPoint, and other publication outputs may provide evidence of distribution, presentation, approval, or delivery, but they do not replace the canonical Markdown source for framework documents unless an approved exception states otherwise.

Supporting evidence shall remain traceable to the applicable authoritative record, activity, decision, approval, or obligation where that relationship is material.

## 8. Evidence Sufficiency, Integrity, Provenance, and Traceability

Evidence shall be sufficient for a reasonable reviewer to understand and evaluate the activity, decision, approval, commitment, exception, change, or outcome it supports.

Evidence quality shall consider:

- Accuracy and completeness appropriate to the purpose.
- Source identity and provenance.
- Date or relevant time context.
- Responsible person, role, client, partner, or business function when applicable.
- Relationship to the supported decision, activity, obligation, or deliverable.
- Version, approval, or change status when material.
- Protection from unauthorized alteration, substitution, or loss.
- Traceability to related records and supporting materials.

Evidence shall not be represented as complete, authoritative, approved, or current when its status is uncertain or unsupported.

## 9. Minimum Record Context

Material records shall preserve enough context to remain understandable and usable after the immediate activity or decision has ended.

Minimum context may include, as applicable:

- Record purpose and category.
- Source or creator.
- Date created, received, approved, changed, or closed.
- Applicable engagement, client, partner, contract, release, decision, or activity.
- Decision, approval, action, exception, or outcome recorded.
- Responsible owner or approving authority.
- Relevant supporting evidence or related records.
- Confidentiality, access, or distribution limitations.
- Retention trigger, preservation need, or disposition restriction when material.

The amount of context required shall be proportionate to the significance, risk, complexity, and expected future use of the record.

## 10. Retention Decision Principles

Retention decisions shall be based on documented and supportable criteria rather than convenience, unlimited storage availability, informal preference, or routine habit.

Retention considerations may include:

- Applicable legal or regulatory obligations identified through appropriate authority or review.
- Contractual, client, partner, insurance, or commercial obligations.
- Governance, approval, exception, and decision-accountability needs.
- Engagement delivery, acceptance, closeout, and dispute considerations.
- Confidentiality, privacy, security, and information-minimization considerations.
- Financial, tax, accounting, or business administration needs.
- Auditability, evidence quality, repeatability, and future review.
- Business continuity, recoverability, and institutional knowledge.
- Known claims, disputes, investigations, incidents, audits, or preservation needs.
- Risk created by continued retention, duplication, access, or disposal.

This policy does not establish fixed retention periods. Specific periods shall be adopted only when supported by an approved obligation, reliable authority, contractual requirement, client requirement, or separately approved retention schedule.

Uncertain or conflicting retention requirements shall be documented and escalated before disposition occurs.

## 11. Preservation and Suspended Disposition

Disposition shall be suspended when a record may be required for an unresolved obligation, dispute, claim, investigation, audit, incident, client request, governance review, contractual requirement, or direction from authorized legal counsel or governance authority.

A preservation need may arise even when the ordinary retention period or business need would otherwise have ended.

When preservation is required:

- The affected record scope shall be identified as clearly as practical.
- Relevant owners and authorized users shall be informed as appropriate.
- Alteration, deletion, destruction, or uncontrolled replacement shall stop.
- Access and confidentiality requirements shall continue to apply.
- The preservation status shall remain until an authorized release or resolution is documented.

This section establishes a governance requirement to preserve records when necessary. It does not create litigation, discovery, investigation, or formal legal preservation procedures.

## 12. Confidentiality, Access, and Need-to-Know

Records shall be protected according to their sensitivity, purpose, source, client relationship, contractual restrictions, and approved business use.

Access shall be limited to persons and parties with a legitimate and authorized need.

Retention does not create an independent right to access, disclose, copy, reuse, publish, or distribute a record.

Records containing client information, confidential information, sensitive information, legal information, commercial information, security information, personal information, or restricted evidence shall remain subject to the Information Handling and Confidentiality Policy and any applicable obligation.

Access uncertainty, unauthorized disclosure, inappropriate reuse, or loss of confidentiality shall be escalated promptly.

## 13. Recoverability and Business Continuity

Required records shall be maintained in a manner that supports reasonable location, retrieval, readability, and recovery for their approved retention period.

Recoverability expectations shall be proportionate to record importance, business dependency, client impact, contractual obligation, evidence value, confidentiality, and continuity needs.

Material records shall not depend solely on an uncontrolled personal copy, temporary location, inaccessible device, unsupported format, or undocumented storage location when loss would create material risk.

This policy defines recoverability expectations only. Detailed backup schedules, recovery procedures, storage architectures, and disaster recovery controls require separate approved standards or procedures.

## 14. Duplication and Over-Retention

Records and evidence shall not be duplicated or retained without a legitimate governance, client, commercial, contractual, operational, continuity, or evidence purpose.

Uncontrolled duplication may create conflicting sources, unauthorized access, confidentiality exposure, obsolete information, unnecessary storage, and inconsistent disposition.

Convenience copies and working materials should be removed when they are no longer needed and when removal is authorized and not subject to a preservation requirement.

Duplicate copies shall not be used to obscure the authoritative record, bypass access restrictions, avoid controlled change, or preserve information that should otherwise be disposed of.

## 15. Periodic Review

Retained records shall be reviewed periodically when continued retention, ownership, access, accuracy, recoverability, or disposition eligibility is uncertain or material.

Review considerations may include:

- Whether the record remains required by an approved obligation or business purpose.
- Whether the authoritative source and owner remain clear.
- Whether preservation remains necessary.
- Whether access remains appropriate.
- Whether unnecessary copies or obsolete working materials exist.
- Whether the record remains readable and recoverable.
- Whether disposition is authorized and appropriate.

Review frequency shall be proportionate to record risk, sensitivity, obligation, importance, change, and expected use. This policy does not prescribe a detailed operational review calendar.

## 16. Authorized Disposition and Disposition Evidence

Disposition shall occur only when the record is no longer required for an approved retention purpose and no preservation requirement applies.

Before material disposition, the responsible owner or authorized function shall consider:

- Applicable retention obligations and documented criteria.
- Open engagements, commitments, claims, disputes, audits, investigations, incidents, or exceptions.
- Client, contractual, commercial, confidentiality, or governance restrictions.
- Whether another authoritative or required record remains available.
- Whether disposition could impair auditability, continuity, accountability, or evidence quality.

Disposition of material or sensitive records shall be authorized and documented at a level proportionate to the record risk and significance.

Disposition evidence shall identify, as applicable, the record category or scope, authorization, date, responsible person or role, and any exception or preservation check.

This policy does not define detailed deletion, destruction, sanitization, media handling, or technical disposition methods.

## 17. Records-Related Risk Escalation

Records-related risk shall be escalated when uncertainty or concern may affect legal or contractual obligations, client trust, confidentiality, commercial commitments, evidence integrity, accountability, recoverability, continuity, or firm reputation.

Escalation conditions may include:

- Missing, lost, corrupted, altered, incomplete, or unrecoverable records.
- Unclear authoritative source or ownership.
- Conflicting retention or disposition requirements.
- Suspected unauthorized access, disclosure, copying, alteration, concealment, or destruction.
- Inability to verify provenance, approval, or traceability.
- Premature or unauthorized disposition.
- Uncontrolled duplication or unnecessary retention of sensitive information.
- A newly identified preservation need.
- A material record maintained only in an unstable, temporary, or inaccessible location.

Escalation shall occur before affected records are altered or disposed of when uncertainty remains material.

## 18. Exceptions

Exceptions to this policy shall be documented, justified, approved by appropriate authority, limited in scope, and reviewed until closed or incorporated into approved governance.

An exception record shall identify:

- The requirement affected.
- The record, category, client, engagement, partner, system, or activity affected.
- The reason for the exception.
- The approving authority.
- The risk created or accepted.
- Applicable compensating actions.
- The duration, review date, or closure condition.

Exceptions shall not be used to bypass applicable law, contractual obligations, client commitments, confidentiality requirements, preservation needs, governance authority, or the Constitution.

## 24. Noncompliance

Failure to follow this policy may create legal, contractual, client trust, confidentiality, commercial, governance, evidence, continuity, operational, or reputational risk.

Material noncompliance may include unauthorized alteration or disposition, failure to preserve required records, inability to recover material evidence, uncontrolled duplication, unsupported record status, unauthorized access, or retention inconsistent with approved requirements.

Material noncompliance shall be documented, escalated, and remediated through an approved corrective action, decision record, release update, exception record, client communication, commercial review, confidentiality review, authorized legal review, or other appropriate governance evidence.

## 25. Review and Maintenance

This policy shall be reviewed when business operations, service offerings, client obligations, contractual obligations, legal requirements, records practices, evidence practices, confidentiality expectations, storage dependencies, recovery needs, or operating practices materially change.

The review shall consider whether additional retention schedules, standards, procedures, classifications, technical controls, or supporting tools are required through a separately approved release.

Approved changes shall follow the controlled governance release process.
